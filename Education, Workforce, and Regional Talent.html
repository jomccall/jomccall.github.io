Education, Workforce, and Regional Talent

An exploration of the data footprint of our region's educational ecosystem.

Updated by Joanna McCall on October 11th, 2021

The Larger Context

The Middle Tennessee workforce is the regional population that is working or actively searching for work. At different points in time, various dynamics in our national and local economies have elevated the search and preparation of the workforce for high demand jobs to different levels. We are currently at a boiling point. The economic conditions that brought us here are described well in this study conducted for the Northern Middle Tennessee Workforce Board by the Nashville Area Chamber of Commerce in 2020:

“In 2007 and again in 2010, the Center for Regional Economic Competitiveness (CREC) completed studies of labor market opportunities for the Nashville area. During this period, the annual unemployment rate went from a low of 5.2% in the Clarksville Metropolitan Statistical Area (MSA) to a height of 10.3%, while the Nashville MSA reached a peak of 9.5% from a low of 4.1%.1 These studies guided regional economic and workforce development efforts, and educational investments, for several years. In 2015, The Research Center of the Nashville Area Chamber of Commerce (Chamber) conducted an updated study that provided data and analysis of industry and occupational sectors driving the region’s jobs growth and prosperity offering insights into Nashville’s demand for workforce supply and identifying skills gaps that posed challenges for future industry growth and development. In 2019, unemployment in the Nashville MSA was trending towards historic lows with some counties dipping below 2.0%, while the nearby Clarksville MSA hovered near 4.0%.2 The economy was at full employment, meaning no workers who are able and willing are involuntarily unemployed.

Meanwhile, the economy (as measured in terms of Gross Domestic Product [GDP]) for both regions continued to grow at a compound annual rate of 3.5% (Clarksville MSA) and 6.1% (Nashville MSA) with jobs for all industries growing at a rate of approximately 3.3% per year.3 The tight labor market, coupled with extraordinary economic growth, did not seem inclined to slow down. This elevated workforce to the region’s top issue, driving policy makers to consider what was needed to develop the talent base, recruit new workforce to the region, and increase workforce participation among Middle Tennesseans through training programs and workforce initiatives. Understanding labor force participation and who remains on the sidelines means consideration of innovations in outreach, training, recruitment practices, and public and private sector policies. Alignment of these systems supports equitable participation in the prosperity of the region.”

To paraphrase this excerpt, the unemployment rate was low, workers had choices, and economists call this a tight labor market. This elevated the conversation about intentional workforce development, bringing new pilot programs and public private partnerships online, bringing grant funding into the region, spurring the creation of internal upskilling programs at various companies, and increasing the work already being done by workforce development professionals of all stripes. Many see this set of conditions as an opportunity to examine opportunities to bring equity into the conversation by targeting those who have been left on the sidelines of our regional economy for pipelines to jobs with family sustaining wages and upward mobility.

Today, the landscape is both similar and different in key ways. A global pandemic came on strong in the region in Spring of 2020. Businesses shut down, people stayed home, and the region saw a tragic death toll of (). Even now, in Autumn of 2021, many industries are operating at diminished capacities, with distancing and other safety measures still in place, or remotely. Layoffs (figures?), raises were put on hold, . What remains the same as the outlook did in early 2020 is the region’s critical need for talent. Manufacturing centers are short, restaurants are understaffed, marketing positions are sitting open for months, while the dynamics and reasons between industries may differ, the need is omnipresent.

This page looks at this need from a few different angles. In the data driven segment the educational ecosystem of the region is examined first, followed by a profile of the area workforce. In the following segment segment some of the work of other organizations are highlighted as examples about what is being done to bridge this gap. This includes work by other researchers, private and public educational institutions, workforce development professionals, workforce boards, foundations and community groups focused on connecting people to good jobs, local governments, private companies, trade groups and unions, and so many more.

On this page we’re looking not only at what educators and training are doing for the workforce, but also at who makes up the workforce. Is participation representative of the area’s population? Does a substantial portion of the workforce have an educational attainment associated with living wages?

The role of this page is to guide you in and around available data that is helpful in assessing the health of the local talent and workforce ecosystem, and direct you to resources that can help enhance understanding and answer your needs.

Highlight Points

Our highlights are broken into three big-picture areas: educational attainment, educational awards, and a workforce demographic profile.

Educational Attainment

A natural place to begin is by exploring the educational attainment of an area. This data is often obtained from the US Census Bureau surveys and that is what we use here. The most reliable data is from the American Community Survey 5-Year Estimates – representing an average over a period of 5 years. This is a good way to examine prevailing trends in an area; almost as if educational attainment is being examined as a demographic feature. The use of 5-year estimates also allows us to use the strength of data collected over 5 years to surpass the weakness of a small geographical sample size, for example, that of a block group. The ACS 5 Year estimates collect information about the following levels of attainment:

  • Less than 9th Grade
  • 9th to 12th Grade, No Degree
  • High School Degree or Equivalent (meaning a GED)
  • Associates Degree
  • Some College No Degree
  • Bachelor’s Degree
  • Graduate Degree
Each of these are associated with different levels of earnings, because they qualify individuals for different types of jobs. The visualization below gives a quick glimpse at the associated earnings in the State of Tennessee from the Census Bureau’s ACS 5 Year Estimates:

Educational Awards

A good way to keep a pulse on the regional pipeline that is more current than the picture you get from Census Educational Attainment data is to examine Educational Awards data. The National Center for Education Statistics is the national reporting body for public and private institutions nationwide.

You are able to obtain a huge amount of information from this agency, including certification awards. These are a good tool for getting people qualified and connected to jobs more quickly and cheaply than four year degrees; appropriate for many jobs in manufacturing, cosmetology, and healthcare. However, the main institutions providing these awards in the Middle Tennessee Region, the Tennessee Colleges of Applied Technology (TCATs), have various different campuses across counties. The NCES data does not allow the viewer to see where these certificates are being awarded, which can affect the efficacy of industry development in the region. The Tennessee Bureau of Regents has supplied GNRC with campus-specific data. The organization standardized their reporting system only this past year, so currently there is only one year of data available broken out by campus. A small visualization can be found below, however a more immersive one is linked at the bottom of this section.

You'll see that the top certificates awarded by all TCAT campuses in the last academic year were: Practical and Vocational Nursing and Nursing Assistants (378 awards), Precision Metal Working (203 awards), Cosmetology (188 awards), and Vehicle Repair and Maintenance Technology (187 awards). These certificates are associated with different occupations providing a variety of wages and typical wrap-around services such as childcare and healthcare benefits. For an exploration of this go to our page exploring living wages here (*LINK*).

For a more immersive group of data visualizations about educational awards, go here: Certifications by TCAT Campuses, where you will find a more in-depth visualization of the data visualized above including certification hierarchy and TCAT campus certification counts, Educational Awards by Tennessee County Annual Breakdowns, where you will find detailed exploratory visualizations of every award level, including certifications, by year for the past decade, and Educational Awards in Tennessee Counties Over Time, where you will find this same data set up for exploratory time-series analysis. The last two visualizations do have TCAT data, however all awards are associated with the parent campus rather than the actual campus location.

Workforce Profile

Here we examine who it is that makes up our workforce; who is and isn't participating, who is moving here, and who may be held back from participation for various reasons.

To begin with, we have an examination of American Community Survey 5-Year Estimates that break down labor force participation by race. Similar to the way we viewed educational attainment above, this is more of a demographic than a current trend. Economic and labor force data is produced more frequently by the Bureau of Labor Statistics than ACS data, often quarterly or monthly, but utilizing the ACS estimates allows us to confidently get a picture of who composes the regional workforce. In other sections we will use BLS and State Department of Labor and Workforce Development data to examine more recent trends.

Technical Notes

Here is where we share the basic Github repositories, the technicalities of survey collection and what that means for how we look at the data, links to all used datasets.

Educational attainment data is collected by the US Census Bureau through both their decennial census and their Current Population Survey. Individuals are asked “What is the highest grade of school ___ has completed, or the highest degree ___ has received?” The question has been incorporated into the Current Population Survey and the Census since 1940, and has been asked the same way as it is today since 1992.
