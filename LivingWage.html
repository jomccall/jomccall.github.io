Living Wage in the Middle Tennessee Region

How do our regional industry and occupational wages serve Middle Tennessee workers and families?

Updated by Joanna McCall on August 27, 2021

The Larger Context

The MIT Living Wage Calculator presents their work by stating that "Analysts and policymakers often compare income to the federal poverty threshold to determine an individual's ability to live within a certain standard of living. However, poverty thresholds do not account for living costs beyond a basic food budget. The federal poverty measure does not consider expenses like childcare and health care that must be covered by a person's income even as these factors affect a person's ability to work and manage hardships associated with balancing employment and other aspects of everyday life. Further, poverty thresholds do not account for geographic variation in the cost of essential household expenses."

The purpose of this portal is to use MIT Living Wage data to examine the the industry and occupational mix in our region in the context of a living wage. This can be used to examine what qualifies as a "good job", it can also be used as an overall metric for the region's wages and thereby quality of life.

MIT breaks all of this information out by family size, allowing a reader to slice data in a variety of ways based on prevailing trends in a region or specific scenarios. We are able to access this information both for the counties that make up our region, and for the region as a whole.

Technical Notes

In order to create the larger source dataset, we are pulling a number different datasets together. First, the information from MIT. Second, industry composition and wages. Third, occupational composition and wages. Finally, family composition per geography from the US Census.

Living Wage Data: For the State of Tennessee

Industry Data: Quarterly Census of Employment and Wages, and GNRC's code for calling this data through the Bureau of Labor Statistics API: Github repository

Occupational Data: This information is only available at a State level from the Bureau of Labor Statistics so special request has to be made from partners with proprietary analytics derived from the QCEW and Industry Occupation Matrix. JobsEQ Analytics via the Nashville Area Chamber of Commerce

Family Composition Data: Census Bureau

Repository for Composition of larger dataset: Github
